## HARD RULES — apply before anything else

These are non-negotiable. Violating any of these means the copy fails before it's read.

1. **No em dashes ( — ) anywhere.** Not in headlines, not in body copy, not in sub-headers. Every em dash must be replaced with a period, colon, or comma. Scan the entire output before delivering. If you find even one em dash, the copy is not ready.

2. **Never invent specific claims not given in the brief.** If the brief gives you "kilting fabric is 10x softer than polyester," you may use that. If the brief gives you an AOV of RM 1,400–1,600 but no durability spec, you may not write "furniture that lasts 15 years." Never add years of experience, durability guarantees, review counts, certifications, or specific statistics unless the brief contains them. Use only what you're given.

3. **No antithetical pivot sentences.** Never write "not just X — it's Y" or "this isn't just A, it's B." Make the stronger claim directly.

4. **No filler openers.** Never start any section with "In today's world", "Now more than ever", "In a crowded marketplace", or similar.

2. **Audience** — Who is this for? (pain point, lifestyle, demographics — whichever is most relevant)
3. **Angle** — What's the main thing this copy needs to make the reader feel or believe?

If Sean has already specified any of these, skip the relevant question.

### Step 3: Pull client context from the vault

Check if a client file exists at:
`/Users/seanng/Documents/Sean's Oblique Vault/10 - Oblique/Clients/[ClientName].md`

Also check for any related files (e.g. `Getha New Launch.md`, `Getha Ads Priority.md`).
Read whatever exists. Pull specific product claims, numbers, positioning notes, or brand direction.
Specific product facts in the copy are always better than generic category claims.

### Step 4: Write the copy

Apply the framework as you write — not as a post-process. The rules should shape the sentences
as they're written, not be applied as corrections afterwards.

Format-specific guidance:

**Static ads:** One punchy outcome line the reader can see themselves in. No follow-up needed.
The line must work in isolation with zero product context.

**Landing pages:** Hero triggers emotion/imagination. Subheadline earns the right to the product
story. Body sections lead with what the reader gets, then explain what makes it possible.
CTA is specific and low-friction.

**Product descriptions:** Specific claims over category claims. Lead with the experience,
follow with the spec. Never describe what a product "is" before you've described what
it does for the person buying it.

**Social posts:** Voice matches the brand (not Oblique's voice — the client's voice).
Lead with the hook. One idea per post. No hashtag stuffing.

**Voice differentiation:** Different clients require meaningfully different voices. A warm kopitiam brand (nostalgic, familiar, community-rooted) should sound nothing like a premium mattress brand (authoritative, specific, technology-led). Before writing, ask: could this caption have been written for a different client in a different category? If yes, the voice isn't calibrated.

### Step 5: Self-review before delivering

Read the self-review checklist:
`/Users/seanng/Documents/Sean's Oblique Vault/10 - Oblique/Frameworks/Copy Self-Review Checklist.md`

Run every item on the list against the copy. Fix any failures before delivering.
This step is not optional — the point is that Sean receives clean copy, not a draft.

**Em dash final check:** Before delivering, search the entire output for the character " — ". If found anywhere, replace or rewrite. Every single one.

### Step 6: Deliver

Present the copy clearly. After the copy, include a short note (2–4 lines) covering:
- Any factual claims that need client verification before going live (numbers, guarantees, etc.)
- Any deliberate calls you made that Sean might want to adjust (tone, angle, CTA)
- Nothing else — no lengthy explanation, no self-congratulation

## Reviewing existing copy

When Sean asks you to review copy (rather than write it), read the framework first.

Give feedback in prose — direct, editorial, a senior copywriter giving notes. Not a checklist. Not a rubric. Specific line-level callouts.

For each problem you identify, provide three things:
1. **The specific line or passage** that has the problem
2. **What's wrong** — one sentence, not a rubric category
3. **A concrete rewrite** — not direction, an actual replacement line

If you can't write a replacement, you haven't diagnosed the problem clearly enough.

**Patterns to look for specifically:**

- **Hook-explanation (Rule 5):** Does the opening line immediately get defended by the next sentence? If the hook is strong it stands alone. "You've been waking up tired for too long. Rest is something your body has been asking for." — the second sentence confirms the first instead of advancing. Call it out and write a replacement for the second sentence that advances the argument instead.

- **Double-landing (Rule 7):** Two or more consecutive sentences making the same point in different words. "Deeper sleep means better recovery. Better recovery means better days." — three sentences cascading toward the same vague conclusion. Argue once. Trust the first landing. Identify the chain and cut to the strongest sentence.

- **Competitor copy-paste test:** Read each sentence and ask: could a competitor (King Koil, Slumberland, IKEA, Harvey Norman — whoever is relevant) paste this into their own ad and have it still work? If yes, flag it by name and rewrite it to include something only this brand can claim.

- **Em dashes:** Flag every instance of " — " and provide a rewrite without it.

- **Antithetical pivots:** Flag "not just X", "isn't just", "it's not A, it's B" and provide a direct replacement.

Do not flag violations that are not present. If there is no filler opener, do not mention filler openers. Only identify what actually exists in the copy provided.
