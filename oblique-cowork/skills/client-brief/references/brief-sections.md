# Master Client Brief — Section Writing Guide

This document provides detailed guidance on what goes in each section and how to write it. Use this alongside the onboarding form data and your research findings.

---

## 1. Cover Page

A clean, minimal cover page that immediately tells the reader what this document is.

**Include:**
- "OBLIQUE" in spaced uppercase (brand identifier)
- "MASTER CLIENT BRIEF" as the main title
- Client name as a subtitle
- Prepared date
- Account Manager name
- Discovery Call date
- "CONFIDENTIAL — FOR INTERNAL OBLIQUE USE ONLY" in red

No body text on the cover page. It's a title page only.

---

## 2. Client Snapshot

A quick-reference overview so anyone picking up this brief can get oriented in 30 seconds.

**Present as a key-value info table:**
- Business Name (include parent company if known from research)
- Industry
- Website
- Business Model (e-commerce, brick & mortar, both, B2B, etc.)
- Revenue Stage
- Geographic Market(s)
- Average Order Value

**Then add two prose subsections:**

### What They Sell
A paragraph describing their product/service range. Lead with the core offering, then expand to the full range. If research uncovered pricing, product launches, or product innovations, weave them in here. This should read like a confident summary from someone who's done their homework — not just a list from the form.

### Business Context (From Research)
A paragraph synthesising what you found from research: brand heritage, recent news, PR coverage, awards, market position, anything that gives the team context beyond what the form provides. Label this clearly as coming from research.

---

## 3. Audience Profile

Build a structured customer avatar from the client's input AND your research. The client's "Who Is Their Customer" and "Best Customer Description" fields are the starting point, but research (especially customer reviews) often reveals language and pain points the client doesn't articulate.

### Demographics
Use bullet points with bold labels. Include: income, ethnicity/market segment (if provided), life stage, location. If the form doesn't specify age range but gives income and lifestyle data, infer a reasonable range and flag it as an assumption in FLAGS & GAPS.

### Psychographics
Write this as a flowing paragraph. Get into the mindset of the customer. What do they value? How do they make decisions? What's their relationship with the product category? Use the client's "Best Customer Description" as a foundation but elevate the language.

### Pain Points
4–5 bullet points. Start with the most acute pain. Draw from both the form and customer review language.

### Desires
4–5 bullet points. What does the ideal customer want? Not just the product — the feeling, the outcome, the identity.

### Language They Use
A paragraph drawing directly from customer reviews, testimonials, and the client's own description. Include actual phrases in quotes. This section is critical for creative teams writing ad copy and social content — it gives them real words to work with.

---

## 4. Brand Positioning

### Current Positioning
A paragraph describing how the brand currently positions itself, based on the form AND website/social research. What's their stated value proposition? What territory do they try to own?

### Brand Personality & Tone
Use the personality descriptors from the form (e.g., "Aspirational, Classy, Authentic, Premium") as bold-label bullet points, with a sentence explaining what each means in practice for this brand. Don't just repeat the adjective — interpret it.

### What They Want to Be Known For
Directly from the form. Keep it short.

### Brand Do's and Don'ts
Two paragraphs with bold "DO:" and "DON'T:" labels. Translate the form's guidelines into actionable creative direction.

### Positioning Gaps Identified (From Research)
This is where your research earns its keep. 3–5 bullet points identifying gaps, risks, or opportunities in how the brand currently presents itself. Examples: follower-to-engagement ratio issues, competitors outpacing them in content, underutilised brand stories, tone mismatches between website and social. Be specific and cite what you found.

---

## 5. Competitive Landscape

### Market Context
Open with a paragraph about the industry landscape — market dynamics, consumer trends, level of competition, key shifts. This comes from research.

### Competitor Table
A bordered table with these columns:
- **Competitor** — Name
- **Positioning** — How they position themselves (one sentence)
- **Strengths** — What they do well (one sentence)
- **Gaps / Weaknesses** — Where they're vulnerable (one sentence)

Include the competitors named in the form PLUS any significant ones you found in research. Aim for 4–6 rows. Each cell should be concise but specific — not generic filler.

### Strategic Opportunity
A paragraph making the case for where the client can win. What positioning territory is unclaimed? What can this client credibly own that no competitor can? This is the most strategic paragraph in the brief — make it count.

---

## 6. Goals & Success Metrics

**Present as a key-value info table:**
- Primary Objective
- Timeline / Urgency
- Target ROAS (if applicable)
- Target CPA (if applicable)
- AOV
- Monthly Ad Budget
- Total Monthly Budget

### What Winning Looks Like
A paragraph translating the dry KPIs into a human narrative. What does success actually look like for this client over the next 6–12 months? Connect the metrics to the business story.

---

## 7. Service Scope

Create one subsection per purchased service. Each subsection follows the same pattern:

### Service Subsection Pattern
1. **Info table** — All relevant service-specific fields from the form
2. **Context paragraph** — What we know about the current state and setup
3. **Campaign / seasonal detail** — Any upcoming dates, launches, peaks
4. **Strategic Note** — An italicised paragraph with your perspective

The strategic note is the most important part. It's where you identify tensions (e.g., "premium brand but aggressive CPA targets"), opportunities (e.g., "dual launch needs two separate funnel strategies"), or priorities (e.g., "KOL budget only supports micro-tier — set expectations accordingly").

### Common Service Types

**Performance Marketing** — Platforms, ad spend, CPA, AOV, ROAS target, funnel setup, existing account maturity, creative assets, seasonal peaks.

**Social Media** — Platforms, posting frequency, who produces content, what's worked, what to avoid, content they admire, upcoming campaigns.

**KOL / Influencer** — Product to promote, existing KOL list, total budget, per-creator budget, creator tiers, platforms, campaign objective, gifting vs paid, brand safety restrictions.

**Web / Development** — Website type, pages needed, who writes copy, who designs, reference sites, integrations, domain/hosting.

**SEO** — Target keywords, priority pages, current SEO status, blog status, competitors to outrank, local vs national, Google Business Profile.

**Brand** — New brand or rebrand, current assets, what's wrong with current brand, brands they admire, founding story, desired customer feeling, deliverables.

If a service type not listed here appears in the form, adapt. The pattern stays the same: info table → context → strategic note.

---

## 8. Key Dates & Constraints

**Present as a key-value info table:**
- Total Monthly Budget
- Monthly Ad Spend (if applicable)
- Immediate Priority / Next Milestone
- Launch Cadence (if relevant)
- Existing Brand Assets available
- Discovery Call Date
- Account Manager

If there's a significant gap between the discovery call date and the brief date, add an italicised note recommending a refresh conversation before execution begins.

---

## 9. Flags & Gaps

This section is mandatory and should be thorough. It protects the team from assumptions and gives the kickoff meeting a clear agenda.

### Missing Information — Confirm at Kickoff
Bullet points listing specific things that are missing from the form or research and need client confirmation. Be precise — not "need more info on social" but "need current Instagram engagement rate, follower growth trend, and top 5 performing posts from the last 3 months."

### Assumptions Made
Bullet points listing every assumption you made while writing the brief. Examples: inferred age range from income data, assumed a tool is a specific brand's competitor when the connection wasn't explicit, assumed a service scope boundary.

### Risks & Concerns
Bullet points with bold labels. Include both risks you identified from research AND concerns flagged by the account manager in the "Flags or Concerns" form field. The AM's concerns are especially important — they've spoken to the client and know where the relationship or operational risks are.

### Upsell Opportunities
Bullet points from the AM's "Upsell Opportunities Identified" field, enriched with your research perspective. If research surfaced additional upsell angles (e.g., a competitor is dominating SEO and the client doesn't have an SEO service), add those too.

---

## 10. Recommended First 30 Days

A numbered list of 5–8 prioritised actions. Each item has:
- **Bold action title** — What to do (e.g., "Lock down April launch details")
- **Description** — Why it matters and what specifically needs to happen (1–2 sentences)

Order by priority — the most urgent or foundational action comes first. The last item should typically be a "plant a seed" action for a future upsell or strategic initiative.

Close the brief with a styled end-line: "End of Brief — Prepared by Oblique Strategy Team, [date]"
