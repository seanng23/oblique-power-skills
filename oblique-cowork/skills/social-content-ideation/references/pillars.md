# The 5-Pillar Content Framework

Oblique's content framework for social media. Every piece of content a client posts should map to one of these five pillars. The framework ensures a brand's social presence has range — it entertains, educates, builds community, tells stories, and humanises the people behind it.

## 1. Entertaining Content

**Purpose:** Stop the scroll, earn shares, grow reach.

**The idea:** Trend-based content that taps into what's currently resonating on social media. The goal is to create something that's relatable, funny, surprising, or emotionally triggering enough that people share it — either by sending it to a friend, reposting to stories, or saving it. This is the pillar that grows your audience because it's the most shareable.

**What this looks like:**
- Trending audio/reel formats adapted to the brand's context
- Relatable moments that the target audience sees themselves in
- Humour that fits the brand's voice (not forced, not cringey)
- Emotional hooks — nostalgia, surprise, satisfaction, "that's so me" moments
- Content that works even if the viewer has never heard of the brand

**Key characteristics:**
- High shareability — the #1 metric for this pillar
- Trend-aware but not trend-desperate. Only use trends that naturally fit the brand. Forcing a trend is worse than skipping it.
- The brand's product or service may be secondary. It's OK if the brand is just the "host" of the entertainment — awareness is the goal.
- Fast-paced, attention-grabbing hooks (first 1-2 seconds matter more here than any other pillar)
- Works across Instagram Reels, TikTok, and Stories

**Example formats:**
- "POV" reels with trending audio
- Relatable memes adapted as video content
- Before/after reveals with satisfying transitions
- "Things only [audience] understand" style content
- Reaction-style content or duets/stitches
- Satisfying process videos (ASMR, cooking, crafting)

**Common mistakes:**
- Copying a trend frame-by-frame without adding brand personality
- Using a trend that peaked 3 weeks ago — by the time it's posted, it feels stale
- Making it too "brand-y" — if it feels like an ad disguised as a trend, it won't get shared
- Ignoring the audio — trending audio is often half the hook

## 2. Educate Content

**Purpose:** Build trust by giving value. Leave people knowing something they didn't before.

**The idea:** Content that teaches the audience something genuinely useful about the brand's domain, products, or industry. This is the pillar that turns casual followers into people who trust the brand — because the brand gave them value without asking for anything in return. The best educational content makes people think "I didn't know that" and then save the post for later.

**What this looks like:**
- "Did you know..." style revelations about the brand's product or industry
- Myth-busting common misconceptions
- Tips, tutorials, and how-tos related to the brand's expertise
- Ingredient/process breakdowns that showcase quality and care
- Behind-the-knowledge content: why the brand does things a certain way

**Key characteristics:**
- Leaves the audience with something they can use, repeat, or share as a fact
- Positions the brand as an authority without being preachy
- The "value" should be specific and surprising — not generic advice anyone could give
- Works especially well as carousels (swipeable tips), talking-head reels, and infographic-style statics
- The educational angle should always connect back to why the brand is credible on this topic

**Example formats:**
- "3 things most people get wrong about [topic]"
- "Why we [do this specific thing] and why it matters"
- "The difference between [common thing] and [what we do]"
- Quick tutorial or recipe using the brand's product
- Ingredient/process spotlight with "did you know" hook
- FAQ-style content answering real customer questions

**Common mistakes:**
- Being too generic — "5 tips for better skin" from a skincare brand is boring. "Why your vitamin C serum turns orange (and what we do differently)" is interesting.
- Lecturing instead of sharing — tone should be conversational, not textbook
- Overloading a single piece of content with too much information
- Not connecting the education back to the brand — educational content should subtly reinforce why this brand is the right choice

## 3. Community Content

**Purpose:** Amplify voices beyond the brand. Build belonging.

**The idea:** Content that showcases the people around the brand — customers, partners, the neighbourhood, the community the brand serves. The whole point is that the brand's social feed shouldn't only feature content made by the marketing team. Real people, real voices, real stories. This is what turns a brand from a company into a community.

**What this looks like:**
- Street interviews asking people questions related to the brand's space
- Customer spotlights and testimonials (but filmed authentically, not corporate)
- UGC (user-generated content) — resharing what customers create about the brand
- "A day in the life of a customer" — following someone's real experience with the brand
- Collaborations with local creators, businesses, or community figures
- Giveaways and interactive challenges that get the audience creating

**Key characteristics:**
- The brand is the platform, not the star. Other people's voices lead.
- Authenticity over production value — overly polished community content feels fake
- Creates a sense of "I could be in this content" for the audience
- Encourages audience participation and two-way interaction
- Builds social proof naturally — seeing real people engage with a brand is more persuasive than any ad

**Example formats:**
- Street interview series ("We asked 10 people in KL...")
- Customer of the week/month spotlight
- "We surprised a loyal customer with..."
- Reposting and reacting to UGC
- Inviting a customer to take over the brand's stories for a day
- Community event coverage
- "Come with us" style content at events or activations

**Common mistakes:**
- Making community content that's actually just testimonials in disguise — people can tell
- Only featuring "perfect" customers — diversity and authenticity matter
- Not actually engaging with UGC when people post it — community is a two-way street
- Staging "real" moments instead of capturing genuine ones

## 4. Storytelling / Cinematic Content

**Purpose:** Elevate brand perception. Create aspirational, memorable content.

**The idea:** This is the premium content pillar. Polished, well-shot, well-edited content that tells a story from the perspective of the target audience or the brand itself. Think mini-films, not social posts. This style of content is extremely strong right now because it stands out in a sea of quick, low-effort content. When done well, it makes people stop and watch the whole thing — and it positions the brand as something worth paying attention to.

**What this looks like:**
- Mini brand films (30-90 seconds) with narrative structure
- Aspirational lifestyle content showing the brand in a desirable context
- Scripted scenarios with a clear beginning, middle, and end
- Cinematic product reveals or launches
- Founder stories or brand origin stories told with production quality
- Day-in-the-life content but shot and edited like a short film

**Key characteristics:**
- **Pre-production is everything.** This pillar requires planning — a script or shot list, location scouting, talent coordination, and editing vision. It's not something you can wing on the day.
- Well-paced — every second earns its place. No filler.
- Strong visual storytelling — the images carry as much meaning as the words
- Emotional resonance — the audience should feel something (aspiration, nostalgia, inspiration, wonder)
- The brand is woven into the story naturally, not forced in as a product plug
- Works best as Instagram Reels, TikTok, and YouTube Shorts (but can also be longer-form)

**Example formats:**
- "A morning with [brand]" — cinematic lifestyle vignette
- Brand story: the journey from start to now, told cinematically
- Product origin story: where does [ingredient/material] come from?
- Seasonal storytelling: connecting the brand to a cultural moment
- Customer transformation story: before and after, told as a narrative
- "What [brand] means to me" — personal essays on video

**Pre-production essentials (for the scripting/storyboarding phase):**
- Script with scene-by-scene breakdown
- Shot list with composition notes
- Location and talent requirements
- Music/sound direction
- Editing style reference (pace, transitions, colour grade)

**Common mistakes:**
- Trying to make cinematic content without planning — it just looks like a regular video with a filter
- Over-scripting so it feels stiff and unnatural
- Making it too long — cinematic doesn't mean long, it means intentional
- Forgetting the story — beautiful shots without narrative purpose are just B-roll
- Neglecting audio design — music, sound effects, and pacing of the voiceover matter as much as the visuals

## 5. EGC — Employee Generated Content

**Purpose:** Humanise the brand from the inside out.

**The idea:** Content created by or featuring the people who work at the brand. Not the marketing team performing — the actual humans behind the scenes. This pillar exists because audiences connect with people, not logos. Seeing a barista talk about their favourite drink, a designer show their workspace, or a founder share a real struggle — that's what makes a brand feel human and trustworthy.

**What this looks like:**
- "Meet the team" introductions (but interesting, not corporate headshots)
- Employee takeover: someone from the team runs the account for a day
- Behind-the-scenes of daily operations, prep work, or creative process
- Team challenges, games, or lighthearted internal content
- Founder or key person sharing honest thoughts, updates, or lessons
- "What I actually do" — a team member explaining their role in a fun way

**Key characteristics:**
- Authentic > polished. This content should feel real, not rehearsed.
- The employees should want to participate — forced participation is obvious and uncomfortable
- Shows company culture genuinely — the good, the quirky, the human
- Builds trust through transparency — "these are the people making your [product]"
- Can overlap with Community (when employees are part of the community story) and Storytelling (when a founder's story is told cinematically)

**⚠️ Important: Not all clients are comfortable with this pillar.** Some businesses prefer to keep their team private, or their employees aren't interested in being on camera. Always flag EGC ideas with a note that this requires client buy-in. Never assume a client is OK with putting their staff on social media.

**Example formats:**
- "A day in my life at [brand]" — employee-filmed
- "Ask the [role]" — customers submit questions, employee answers
- Team taste tests, challenges, or reactions
- Behind-the-scenes prep (kitchen, studio, workshop, warehouse)
- "Things customers don't know about [what we do]"
- Quick team introductions with personality questions

**Common mistakes:**
- Making it too corporate — "Hi, I'm Sarah from Marketing and I love working at [brand]!" is not EGC, it's an HR video
- Pressuring employees who aren't comfortable on camera
- Over-editing and removing the authenticity
- Only featuring management — show the whole team
- Not considering cultural sensitivity around showing employees (especially in some industries/regions)

## Pillar Distribution Guide

The right pillar mix depends on the client. Use these as starting frameworks:

**New/early-stage brand (building awareness):**
- Entertaining: 4 | Educate: 3 | Community: 2 | Storytelling: 2 | EGC: 1

**Established brand (deepening engagement):**
- Entertaining: 2 | Educate: 3 | Community: 3 | Storytelling: 2 | EGC: 2

**Premium/luxury positioning:**
- Entertaining: 1 | Educate: 2 | Community: 2 | Storytelling: 4 | EGC: 3

**Personality-driven brand (strong founder):**
- Entertaining: 2 | Educate: 2 | Community: 2 | Storytelling: 3 | EGC: 3

These are guides, not formulas. The actual mix should be informed by the brand research.

## Cross-Pillar Thinking

Some ideas naturally blend pillars. A few common overlaps:

- **Entertaining + Educate**: "Myth-busting" content that's presented in a fun, trend-driven format
- **Community + EGC**: Employees interviewing customers, or team members participating in community events
- **Storytelling + EGC**: A founder's story told with cinematic production quality
- **Educate + Storytelling**: A beautifully shot "how it's made" or ingredient origin story

When an idea spans two pillars, assign it to the one that's most dominant in the execution. A myth-busting reel using a trending format? That's Entertaining first, Educate second. A cinematic founder interview? That's Storytelling first, EGC second.
