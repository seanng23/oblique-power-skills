# Daniel Priestley's Framework Creation Methodology

Source: LinkedIn video — Daniel Priestley on developing frameworks as intellectual property.

---

## The Core Premise

A framework is an underlying structure that does the heavy lifting for any big idea. It helps structure thinking, makes ideas teachable, and — when done well — becomes intellectual property that can power the growth of a business.

Priestley's examples:
- **Stephen Covey** — was a church-connected SME consultant. Noticed some clients were highly effective, others were all talk. Documented the difference as "The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People." Book sold 20M+ copies. Built a $400M+ consulting business on those frameworks.
- **Simon Sinek** — worked with businesses in London and New York. Noticed companies with strong, articulated purpose outperformed those without. Created "Start With Why" (Why → What → How concentric circles). Book + $50M+ consulting business.

Both were normal consultants who stopped, reflected, documented their observations into frameworks, and became famous for them.

---

## The Extraction Process

### Step 1: Ask the Powerful Question

> "When did I do something special that got a measurable outcome, I can explain it step by step, and it would benefit a certain type of person?"

All four criteria must be met:
1. **Something special** — not routine, a standout result
2. **Measurable outcome** — numbers, concrete results
3. **Explainable step by step** — you can articulate the process
4. **Benefits a certain type of person** — there's an audience for this knowledge

### Step 2: Build a Timeline of Examples

Look back 3-5 years. Go through calendars, photos, case studies. Find 4-5 distinct examples that meet the criteria. Document each as a mini case study.

### Step 3: Identify the Pattern

Across the examples, what was consistent? What steps repeated? What principles held true every time?

### Step 4: Choose a Framework Format

Priestley identifies three primary formats:

**1. A List (Steps / Principles / Processes)**
- The most common and accessible format
- Odd numbers work best for memorability: 3, 5, 7, 9
- Examples: 7 Habits, 5 Love Languages, 4-Hour Work Week

**2. A Diagram**
- Concentric circles (like Start With Why)
- Venn diagrams (overlapping domains)
- Quadrants / 2x2 matrices (like Cashflow Quadrant)
- Target diagrams
- Visual representation of relationships between elements

**3. A Signature Story**
- A story told repeatedly that reveals a truth or framework
- Has structure that helps people make better decisions
- Often the story IS the teaching tool

Best practice: blend all three. A framework with steps + a diagram + a signature story is more complete and memorable than any single format.

### Step 5: Make It Memorable

Two key techniques:
- **Alliteration** — words starting with the same letter (e.g. Priestley's own "Name, Same, Claim to Fame, Aim in a Game")
- **Acronyms** — the methodology spells out a relevant word

These make frameworks stickier and more shareable.

## Why Frameworks Matter (Priestley's Argument)

- Frameworks become intellectual property — a business asset
- They can be used internally (team alignment, consistent processes) or externally (marketing, thought leadership, attracting clients)
- Entrepreneurs who fail often don't pause to formalise their frameworks
- The businesses that scale on knowledge (consulting, education, agencies) are almost always built on signature frameworks
- Frameworks are the reason people seek you out specifically, rather than any competitor

---

## Key Insight for Application

The skill of building frameworks is not about inventing something from nothing. It's about noticing what you're already doing that works, extracting the pattern, and giving it structure, a name, and a visual form. The raw material already exists in your experience.
